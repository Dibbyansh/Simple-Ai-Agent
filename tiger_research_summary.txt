Tiger (Panthera tigris) Research Summary

**Species Overview**
The tiger (Panthera tigris) is a large cat and member of the genus Panthera. It is one of the world's most iconic big cats, with distinctive orange coats marked by black stripes. Tigers are apex predators and play a crucial role in maintaining healthy ecosystems.

**Subspecies and Distribution**
Historically, tigers ranged across Asia from the Caucasus to Indonesia. Currently, they are found in fragmented populations across 13 countries: India, Bangladesh, Nepal, Bhutan, China, Myanmar, Thailand, Malaysia, Indonesia (Sumatra), Russia, Cambodia, Laos, and Vietnam. There are several recognized subspecies:
- Bengal tiger (P. t. tigris) - most numerous, primarily Indian subcontinent
- Amur/Siberian tiger (P. t. altaica) - largest subspecies, males up to 660 lbs, found in Russia/Far East
- Sumatran tiger (P. t. sondaica) - smallest subspecies, males ~310 lbs, critically endangered
- Indochinese tiger (P. t. corbetti) - critically endangered
- Malayan tiger (P. t. jacksoni) - critically endangered
- South China tiger (P. t. amoyensis) - possibly extinct in the wild (none observed since 1970s)
- Extinct subspecies: Bali (P. t. balica), Caspian (P. t. virgata), Javan (P. t. sondaica) - all became extinct in the 20th century

**Physical Characteristics**
- Size varies significantly by subspecies and sex; males are larger than females
- Largest: Amur tiger males up to 660 pounds, females up to 370 pounds
- Smallest: Sumatran tiger males around 310 pounds
- Lifespan: up to 20 years in the wild

**Behavior and Ecology**
- Mostly solitary except for mother-cub bonds
- Large territories marked with urine, feces, scrapes, and vocalizations
- Territory size depends on prey availability
- Apex predators, primarily hunting large ungulates
- Strong swimmers, often using water for hunting and cooling

**Reproduction and Life Cycle**
- Sexual maturity: females at 3-4 years, males at 4-5 years
- Independence: around 2 years of age
- High juvenile mortality: about 50% of cubs die before age 2
- Females give birth to 2-4 cubs after ~3.5 month gestation

**Conservation Status**
- IUCN Red List: Endangered overall
- Subspecies status: Malayan, Indochinese, and Sumatran subspecies are Critically Endangered
- South China tiger likely extinct in the wild
- Range has declined by 53% to 68% since the late 1990s

**Major Threats**
- Habitat destruction and fragmentation from deforestation
- Poaching for fur and illegal trade of body parts (used in traditional medicine)
- Human-wildlife conflict and retaliatory killings
- Competition for space with growing human populations

**Population and Trends**
- Current estimate (2024): approximately 3,900 to 5,574 wild tigers globally
- Global Tiger Forum (2025) reported 5,574 wild tigers
- This represents an increase from historical lows but remains far below historical numbers
- TX2 initiative (2010-2022) aimed to double wild tiger populations; populations have increased in Bhutan, China, India, Nepal, and Russia

**Conservation Efforts**
- Protected areas and wildlife corridors
- Anti-poaching patrols and law enforcement
- Community engagement to reduce human-tiger conflict
- International cooperation through Global Tiger Forum
- Captive breeding programs for potential reintroduction
